The Promise The Problem Take Action

1Iowa made a promise, and made it the right way.

When Russia invaded Ukraine, millions of families lost their homes to bombs and occupation overnight. DeWitt led the way with churches opening their doors. Neighbors raised roughly half a million dollars of their own money (Iowa PBS; Welcome.US), and everyday citizens signed a legal promise to be responsible for specific, named Ukrainian families fleeing Russia's war (National Immigration Forum).

The families were vetted overseas and came through the front door (IRC). No visa, no green card, no handouts. Just temporary permission to be here with one renewable work permit.

2Both sides kept it, and everyone won.

DeWitt and IA NICE built a system that gets a family functional and on its feet in months: more than 20 families welcomed, every one financially independent, most within about four months (WQAD). They worked, paid taxes, and bought homes and cars.

In a town of 5,000, those 65 newcomers are over 1% growth, the equivalent of Chicago adding 30,000 people, growth most rural towns can only dream of (ISU Rural Iowa at a Glance). And they are exactly the workforce our short-handed towns needed.

DeWitt proved the model works, and the towns and communities across the state started to copy it. All Iowa ever needed from Washington was for the paperwork to keep moving.

3Washington is about to break what Iowa built.

In two years, Washington has terminated sponsorship programs and ended protections country after country (NBC News), and a rule proposed June 5 now takes aim at the work permit itself (Federal Register).

Working families would have to prove they need to work. Permits would expire every year, throwing every family back into the backlog. And renewals would require an employer enrolled in E-Verify: the diner, the family farm, and the machine shop mostly never enrolled, and a trucker who owns his own rig has no employer to enroll at all. A family can do everything right and still lose its permit because of where they work.

Now watch what one dead permit does to a town.

  • The income stops, but the mortgage and car loan do not.
  • The local bank watches its loans slide toward default.
  • The employer loses a trained worker.
  • The school loses the enrollment that keeps its doors open.

And the Iowans who signed the promise inherit the hardship: months of paperwork limbo, renewals, and mazes they now have to walk these families through, sharing the strain of a stopped income because they gave their word and they mean it.

If a promise can be turned into a nightmare, no Iowan signs again, no church opens its doors again, and the solution DeWitt built and proved dies under the sledgehammer.

Whatever your politics, this is the model you asked for: legal, vetted, privately funded, self-sufficient in months, and locally run. The only thing it cannot survive is a work permit that stops working.
